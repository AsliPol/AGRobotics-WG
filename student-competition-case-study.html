Case Study · AgRobotics Working Group

Student Competition —
How I Would Build It

I haven't worked at AgRobotics WG yet. But when I saw the Student Competition in the job description, something clicked.

01 — The situation

A first-edition event. No playbook. No template. No one who has done it before to ask.

For some people, that sounds stressful. For me, it sounds familiar.

At Meeting Point Turkey, I was responsible for tracking over 500,000 monthly reservations across three international partners. Some months, the numbers didn't add up. Large discrepancies, no obvious explanation, pressure from all sides. I learned quickly that panicking doesn't close the gap. What closes the gap is sitting down, cleaning the data, finding where things broke, and walking into your supervisor's office with a solution in your hand — not just a problem.

Building this competition from zero is the same muscle. You don't wait for someone to hand you a checklist. You figure out the first step, you take it, and you write everything down so the person after you has an easier time.

This is how I would build it.

02 — My approach
01
Outreach & stakeholder mapping
Building the network from zero
+
  • Map universities and colleges with agriculture, engineering, and technology programs across Ontario — University of Guelph, Ridgetown, Niagara College, Fanshawe, and others
  • Identify faculty contacts and student agriculture clubs — build a contact database in Excel with name, institution, email, and follow-up status
  • Draft a clear one-page brief explaining the competition: what it is, who it's for, what students gain, and how to apply
  • Send outreach via email (Mailchimp) and LinkedIn — track open rates and responses to know what's working
  • Follow up personally with any non-responses after two weeks
Everything documented — so year two starts with a warm list, not a cold start.
02
Competition framework
Criteria · judging · submission process
+
  • Define the challenge brief: a real problem from the AgRobotics ecosystem that students solve with a proposal or prototype
  • Build a judging rubric — scored on innovation, feasibility, agriculture relevance, and presentation quality
  • Recruit a balanced panel of judges: one producer, one tech company representative, one academic, one from RHA Ventures
  • Set up a simple submission process — Google Form for entries, tracking spreadsheet for status (received / under review / shortlisted / finalist)
  • Create a judge briefing document so every judge scores consistently
The rubric and briefing document become the permanent framework — reusable every year with minor updates.
03
Marketing & communications
Canva · Mailchimp · social media
+
  • Design a visual identity for the competition in Canva — consistent with AgRobotics WG branding: logo, colours, fonts
  • Create a content calendar: 6 weeks of posts covering announcement, judge spotlights, application deadline reminders, finalist reveals, and winner announcement
  • Build the email campaign in Mailchimp — launch email, mid-campaign reminder, finalist announcement, post-event recap
  • Graphic assets for each stage: save-the-date card, application open post, judge introduction cards, winner announcement graphic
  • Track engagement with Google Analytics — which posts drove the most application clicks, which emails had the best open rate
Every graphic asset saved in a shared folder, labelled by stage — so next year's coordinator can update the date and post.
04
Event day & documentation
Run-of-show · post-event report
+
  • Build a minute-by-minute run-of-show document — who is where, what happens when, who is the backup if something goes wrong
  • Prepare all materials in advance: printed judging sheets, name tags, schedules for presenters, sponsor acknowledgements
  • Capture content on the day — photos, quotes from participants and judges, key moments for post-event social posts
  • Send a post-event survey to participants and judges within 48 hours — collect feedback on what worked and what to improve
  • Write a post-event report: attendance numbers, application volume, social media reach, survey results, and three recommendations for year two
The post-event report is the most important deliverable. It is the playbook for the next edition.
